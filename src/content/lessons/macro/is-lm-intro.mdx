## Goods market: the IS curve

The IS curve traces combinations of output $Y$ and the real interest rate $r$
that clear the goods market. Starting from the national-income identity in a
closed economy,

$$
Y = C(Y - T) + I(r) + G,
$$

and assuming a linear consumption function $C = C_0 + c(Y - T)$ with a
tax rate $T = tY$ and investment $I = I_0 - b r$, solving for $Y$ gives the IS
relation

$$
Y_{IS}(r) \;=\; \frac{1}{1 - c(1-t)} \big(C_0 + I_0 + G - b\,r\big).
$$

The slope is negative: a lower interest rate raises investment and, through the
multiplier $\alpha = 1/(1 - c(1-t))$, raises equilibrium output.

## Money market: the LM curve

Money-market equilibrium sets real money demand equal to real money supply:

$$
\frac{M}{P} = k Y - h r \quad\Longrightarrow\quad r_{LM}(Y) = \frac{k Y - M/P}{h}.
$$

LM is upward-sloping in $(Y, r)$: higher output raises transactions demand for
money, requiring a higher interest rate to clear the market at fixed $M/P$.

## Equilibrium

Setting $Y_{IS}(r) = Y$ and $r_{LM}(Y) = r$ and solving simultaneously:

$$
Y^\* = \frac{\alpha h A + b(M/P)}{h + \alpha b k}, \qquad
r^\* = \frac{k Y^\* - M/P}{h},
$$

where $A = C_0 + I_0 + G$.

## Comparative statics — try to predict before you drag

1. **Fiscal expansion** (↑ G): IS shifts right ⇒ $Y^\*$ up, $r^\*$ up. The rise
   in $r$ partially crowds out investment.
2. **Monetary expansion** (↑ M): LM shifts right ⇒ $Y^\*$ up, $r^\*$ down.
3. **Confidence shock** (↑ $C_0 + I_0$): IS shifts right ⇒ both $Y^\*$ and
   $r^\*$ rise.
