## What is a stock worth?

A share of stock is a claim on the future dividends the firm chooses
to pay you, plus whatever you can sell the share for later. Since
"sell it later" itself depends on someone else's view of future
dividends, the share's intrinsic value reduces to the PV of all
future dividends. That's the dividend discount model (DDM):

$$
P_0 = \sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \frac{D_t}{(1 + r_E)^t}.
$$

Where $D_t$ is the dividend at time $t$ and $r_E$ is the cost of
equity (covered in Unit 4). Until then, treat $r_E$ as given.

## The Gordon special case

If dividends grow at constant rate $g$ forever, the infinite sum
collapses to:

$$
P_0 = \frac{D_1}{r_E - g}.
$$

Requires $g < r_E$. Works for mature, stable companies (utilities,
consumer staples) where payout policy is set and growth is predictable.

Example. Coca-Cola pays a \$1.88 annual dividend, has historically
grown it about 5% per year, and has a cost of equity of about 8%.

$$
P_0 = \frac{1.88 \times 1.05}{0.08 - 0.05} = \frac{1.974}{0.03} \approx \$65.80.
$$

Coca-Cola actually trades around \$60-70 per share in 2025-2026. The
Gordon model gets us in the ballpark precisely because Coke is the
kind of company it was designed for.

## When Gordon breaks

Gordon assumes a single constant $g$ forever. Lots of real firms
don't fit that.

- **High-growth firms** (early Amazon, Nvidia today): $g > r_E$ for
  some period, so the formula gives infinity. Need multi-stage.
- **Cyclical firms**: dividend growth is anything but constant.
- **No-dividend firms** (most tech in their growth phase): $D = 0$
  forever, so DDM says the stock is worth \$0$. Obviously wrong.

## Multi-stage DDM

Split the firm's life into two (or more) phases:

1. **Explicit forecast** (years 1 to $T$): forecast each year's
   dividend individually.
2. **Terminal value** (year $T+1$ onwards): apply Gordon at a
   sustainable long-run growth rate.

$$
P_0 = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{D_t}{(1+r_E)^t} + \frac{1}{(1+r_E)^T} \cdot \frac{D_{T+1}}{r_E - g_{LR}}.
$$

The first term is the PV of forecasted dividends. The second is the
PV of the Gordon terminal value, discounted back to today.

Example. A firm pays $D_1 = \$2$, growing 12% per year for 5 years,
then 3% per year forever. $r_E = 10\%$.

Years 1-5 dividends: 2.00, 2.24, 2.51, 2.81, 3.15. PV at 10%:
sum to about \$9.46.

Year 6 dividend = 3.15 × 1.03 = 3.24. Terminal value at end of year
5: \$3.24 / (0.10 - 0.03) = \$46.32$. PV today: \$46.32 / 1.10^5 \approx \$28.76$.

Stock value: \$9.46 + 28.76 = \$38.22 per share.

## FCFE: when there are no dividends to discount

For firms that don't pay dividends (most growth-stage tech), use
**free cash flow to equity**:

$$
\text{FCFE} = \text{Net Income} + \text{D\&A} - \Delta\text{WC} - \text{CapEx} + \text{Net Borrowing}.
$$

This is the cash available to equity holders after the firm has paid
for operations, working capital, CapEx, and net debt repayments. If
the firm doesn't pay it all out as a dividend, that cash either
accumulates as additional book value or funds buybacks — either way
it accrues to equity holders.

Equity value = PV of FCFE discounted at cost of equity:

$$
\text{Equity value} = \sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \frac{\text{FCFE}_t}{(1+r_E)^t}.
$$

Per-share value = Equity value / shares outstanding.

This is the right approach for non-dividend payers because what
matters is the cash the firm *could* pay out, not the cash it
*chooses* to pay out.

## DDM vs FCFE: which when

| Use DDM when | Use FCFE when |
|---|---|
| Firm has stable dividend policy | Firm pays little or no dividend |
| Payout ratio is predictable | Payout is sporadic / opportunistic |
| You're valuing utility-like, consumer-staple, mature financial firms | You're valuing tech, growth, M&A targets |

For most large public US firms today, FCFE is the more general tool.
DDM remains useful as a sanity check and for traditional dividend-
focused investors.

## A reconciliation

If a firm pays out 100% of FCFE as dividends, FCFE = dividends and
the two methods give identical equity values. The DDM is a special
case of FCFE valuation under a specific (and often unrealistic)
payout policy. Knowing this means you don't need to memorize "which
method": pick the one whose inputs you can defend.
