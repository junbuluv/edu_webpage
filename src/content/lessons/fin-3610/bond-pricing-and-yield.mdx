## A bond is just an annuity plus a lump sum

A standard coupon bond has three numbers:

- **Face value** $F$ (also called par or principal), typically \$1,\!000 for US corporates.
- **Coupon rate** $c$ (annual rate, paid as $c \cdot F / 2$ semiannually in the US; we'll use annual coupons here for simplicity).
- **Maturity** $T$ years.

It pays $c \cdot F$ each year for $T$ years, plus $F$ at maturity.
Its price is the PV of those cash flows at the yield to maturity $y$:

$$
P = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{c \cdot F}{(1+y)^t} + \frac{F}{(1+y)^T}.
$$

The first term is a coupon annuity; the second is a lump-sum face
value. Both formulas you've already seen.

## YTM = IRR of a bond

The **yield to maturity** is the single discount rate that makes
the bond's PV equal to its current market price. It's the bond
investor's IRR if held to maturity, assuming all coupons are
reinvested at $y$.

Given a price, you solve for $y$, usually iteratively, or with a
spreadsheet's RATE function.

## Three regimes

| Coupon vs YTM | Price vs Face | Name                      |
| ------------- | ------------- | ------------------------- |
| $c > y$       | $P > F$       | trading at a **premium**  |
| $c = y$       | $P = F$       | trading at **par**        |
| $c < y$       | $P < F$       | trading at a **discount** |

A bond issued at par at a 5% coupon, then yields rise to 7%: the price
falls below par. Why would anyone pay par for a 5% coupon when the
market wants 7%? They wouldn't. So the price drops to make the
_total_ return (coupons + capital gain at maturity) equal 7%.

Three things to confirm:

1. **Price-yield is downward-sloping and convex.** As yield rises,
   price falls, at a decreasing rate (the convexity).
2. **Longer maturity = steeper curve.** A 30-year bond's price is
   far more sensitive to yield changes than a 2-year bond's.
3. **Zero-coupon bonds are the most sensitive of all.** Toggle the
   "zero-coupon" checkbox and watch the curve get even steeper:
   100% of the cash flow comes at maturity, so duration equals
   maturity.

## Duration: how price-sensitive

Two duration concepts you should know:

**Macaulay duration** $D_{\text{Mac}}$ is the weighted-average time
until you receive the bond's cash flows, with weights equal to each
cash flow's share of the bond's total PV:

$$
D_{\text{Mac}} = \frac{1}{P} \sum_{t=1}^{T} t \cdot \frac{C_t}{(1+y)^t}.
$$

**Modified duration** $D_{\text{mod}} = D_{\text{Mac}} / (1 + y)$ is
the one that's actually useful for risk management. It estimates the
percentage price change for a small yield change:

$$
\frac{\Delta P}{P} \approx -D_{\text{mod}} \cdot \Delta y.
$$

Example. A 10-year bond at 5% YTM with a 5% coupon has Macaulay
duration ≈ 8.1 years and modified duration ≈ 7.7. If yields jump
50 bps (from 5% to 5.5%), the bond price falls by approximately
$7.7 \times 0.005 = 3.85\%$.

This is the **interest-rate risk** banks and bond funds manage every
day. Silicon Valley Bank's failure in 2023 was largely a duration
mismatch story: they held long-duration bonds and Treasuries, and
when yields spiked in 2022-2023, the mark-to-market value of those
holdings fell sharply, eroding their capital.

## Why convexity matters

Modified duration is a _linear_ approximation. For small yield
moves it's accurate; for large moves, the price-yield curve is
visibly bent, so the linear approximation under-estimates how much
price falls when yields rise and how much it gains when yields
fall. The bend is **convexity**, and it's a positive thing for the
bondholder: they benefit asymmetrically.

For most coursework you can treat convexity as a second-order
correction; for real-world risk management of large portfolios, it's
material.

## Spreads come next

So far we've assumed a single risk-free yield $y$. Corporate bonds
trade at higher yields than Treasuries: the difference is the
**credit spread**, which compensates for default risk. That's the
